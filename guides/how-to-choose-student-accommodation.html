How to Choose Student Accommodation

A practical guide to help students and parents choose safe, affordable and study-friendly student accommodation near UFS and CUT in Bloemfontein.

Choosing Accommodation Is a Serious Decision

Student accommodation is not just a place to sleep. It affects your safety, transport costs, study habits, monthly budget, independence, friendships and overall university experience.

Many students choose accommodation too quickly because they are under pressure before registration. Others choose based only on price or because their friends are staying somewhere. That can lead to problems later.

The best student accommodation should support your academic life, protect your wellbeing and make your daily routine easier.

Tip: Before choosing accommodation, compare location, safety, cost, WiFi, house rules, transport access and whether the place suits your funding requirements.

1. Choose the Right Location

Location is one of the most important factors. A room can look affordable, but if it is far from campus, transport costs and travel stress can become a problem.

Students studying at UFS or CUT should think carefully about how far they will travel every day, how safe the route is and whether the area is suitable for student living.

2. Prioritise Safety

Safety should never be ignored. Students should feel comfortable where they live, and parents should be able to trust that the accommodation environment is properly managed.

Before choosing accommodation, ask how access is controlled, what security measures exist and what rules protect students.

Access Control

Understand who can enter the property and how access is managed.

Lighting

Well-lit spaces help students feel safer when moving around.

Emergency Contacts

Students should know who to contact when there is a problem.

Clear Rules

Rules help manage visitors, noise, respect and student behaviour.

3. Check the Total Cost

Do not compare accommodation only by monthly rent. Always ask what the price includes and what students must still pay separately.

A cheaper room may become expensive if the student must still pay for transport, WiFi, electricity, laundry or other essentials.

  • Monthly rental amount.
  • Deposit or once-off fees.
  • Electricity and water arrangements.
  • WiFi costs.
  • Laundry access and costs.
  • Transport costs to campus.
  • Food and grocery costs.
  • Damage or breakage rules.
  • Payment deadlines.
  • Move-in and move-out terms.

5. Check WiFi and Study Conditions

Reliable internet is important for university students. Many assignments, lectures, research tasks and communication channels require online access.

A good student room should also support studying. Students need enough space for a laptop, books, notes and quiet study time.

  • Ask whether WiFi is included.
  • Ask about internet reliability.
  • Check whether the room has a desk or study area.
  • Ask about noise rules.
  • Choose an environment that supports focus.
  • Avoid accommodation where studying will be difficult.

6. Understand the House Rules

House rules are important because they protect students and keep the property organised. Good accommodation should have clear expectations.

Before signing or moving in, ask about:

Visitors

Understand visitor times and visitor limits.

Noise

Ask about quiet hours and study-friendly rules.

Cleaning

Understand cleaning responsibilities for shared spaces.

Payments

Know payment dates, penalties and proof requirements.

7. Compare Private Accommodation and Residence

Some students choose university residence. Others prefer private student accommodation. Each option can work depending on the student’s personality, study needs and budget.

University residence may offer a more structured campus-linked environment. Private accommodation may offer more independence, privacy and choice.

8. Confirm NSFAS Accreditation or Approval

If you are funded by NSFAS, accommodation approval is very important. Students should confirm whether the accommodation is accredited or approved where required before making final decisions.

NSFAS-funded students should keep copies of accommodation documents, proof of registration and all communication related to accommodation.

9. Visit or View the Accommodation Properly

If possible, students or parents should view the accommodation before moving in. If an in-person visit is not possible, ask for clear photos, videos and written details.

  • Check the room condition.
  • Look at shared spaces.
  • Ask about bathrooms and kitchen access.
  • Check doors, windows and locks.
  • Ask about WiFi and electricity.
  • Confirm furniture included.
  • Ask about move-in procedures.
  • Make sure the address is clear.

10. Read the Agreement Carefully

Before paying or moving in, read the accommodation agreement or lease carefully. Students and parents should understand the terms before committing.

Payment Terms

Understand rent, due dates, deposits and penalties.

House Rules

Know what behaviour is allowed and not allowed.

Move-Out Terms

Understand notice periods and end-of-stay rules.

Damage Policy

Know who pays for damage and how it is handled.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important thing when choosing student accommodation?
Safety, location, affordability and study environment are among the most important factors. Students should not choose accommodation based on price alone.
Should I choose accommodation near campus?
Accommodation near campus can reduce transport costs, save time and make it easier to attend lectures, tutorials and campus activities.
How do I know if accommodation is suitable for students?
Check the location, safety measures, house rules, WiFi, study space, transport access, payment terms and overall living environment.
Can NSFAS students choose private accommodation?
NSFAS-funded students may use approved or accredited private accommodation where the required processes are followed.
What should parents ask before choosing accommodation?
Parents should ask about safety, location, payment terms, house rules, emergency contacts, what is included and whether the accommodation is suitable for their child’s studies.
